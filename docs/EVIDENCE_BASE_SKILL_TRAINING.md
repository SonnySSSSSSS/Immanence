# Evidence Base: Meditation As Skill Training

This document summarizes the current evidence for framing Immanence as a skill-training system.
It is not a clinical treatment claim document.

## Positioning Summary

- Best-supported framing: meditation as trainable mental fitness (attention, awareness, emotional regulation).
- Typical effect sizes are small-to-moderate in many studies.
- Active-control comparisons usually reduce effect size versus waitlist controls.
- Evidence is strongest for functional and behavioral outcomes, weaker for strong structural brain claims at short app-level doses.

## Performance Reliability Model

Immanence uses a performance reliability framing for cognitive claims:

- Not a claim of higher intelligence or a larger cognitive ceiling.
- A claim of more consistent access to existing capacity.
- Less attentional dropout and less drift under load.
- Better return-to-task after distraction.

Practical analogy:

- Similar to the difference between good sleep and poor sleep before a difficult task.
- The range may be similar, but day-to-day and moment-to-moment access to that range is more stable.

Evidence alignment:

- This framing is better supported than strong "cognitive enhancement" claims.
- It aligns with studies showing reduced mind wandering, preserved performance under stress, and improved attentional allocation efficiency.

## High-Confidence Claims (Safe Public Use)

1. Focused attention practice can improve concentration and reduce mind wandering with consistent training.
2. Open awareness practice can improve meta-awareness and decentering (observing thoughts without automatic reaction).
3. Mindfulness-style training can reduce stress and anxiety in healthy populations.
4. Attention and emotional regulation behave like trainable skills that improve with repeated practice.
5. Different meditation families appear to train different capacities (for example concentration, decentering, compassion).

## Medium-Confidence Claims (Use Careful Wording)

1. Meditation practice is associated with measurable brain changes in regions linked to attention and regulation.
2. Some benefits may persist outside active practice sessions.
3. Intensive training can preserve attentional performance under stress.

Use wording such as "associated with," "may help," and "research suggests."

## Claims To Avoid

1. "Meditation treats or cures depression, anxiety, PTSD, or other disorders."
2. "Meditation rewires your brain" (as a guaranteed or permanent outcome).
3. "Meditation can replace therapy or medication."
4. "X minutes of meditation equals Y hours of sleep/rest."
5. "Meditation boosts immunity" as a broad guaranteed outcome.
6. "Meditation grows gray matter" as a direct app-level claim.
7. Universal claims such as "works for everyone" or guaranteed outcomes.

## Evidence Caveats

1. Effects are often smaller against active controls than against inactivity controls.
2. Publication bias and selective reporting remain concerns in the field.
3. Long-term dose-response and maintenance effects are still under-studied.
4. Open monitoring has a smaller direct evidence base than focused attention in many trial sets.
5. Structural neuroplasticity claims from short interventions are contested.

## Recommended Public Claim Style

- Prefer: "Immanence trains attention, awareness, and regulation skills."
- Prefer: "Many users may experience improved focus and reduced reactivity with consistent practice."
- Prefer: "This is a training tool, not medical treatment."
- Avoid certainty language such as "will," "guarantees," or "cures."
